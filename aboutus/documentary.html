What is junk mail or e-waste?

Electronic waste, also known as e-waste, is made up of products that have no more value due to lack of use, replacement or breakage. The category includes “white goods” such as refrigerators, washing machines and microwaves, as well as electronic devices such as televisions, computers, cell phones, tablets, drones, as well as batteries, cartridges and toners. The destination of waste has become a global challenge. Brazil holds the title of seventh largest producer of electronic waste in the world, behind China, the United States, Japan, India, Germany and the United Kingdom, respectively.




E-waste in Brazil

Brazil is the leader in Latin America in the production of electronic waste, a position that no place would like to be. According to a study by the Global e-Waste Monitor carried out by the United Nations (UN), the country annually produces 1.5 tons of e-waste and only 3% is properly collected to be recycled or disposed of properly. The equipment has a chemical composition with substances that are highly toxic to the environment and its decomposition can cause great harm to human health. Is throwing cell phones in the trash an environmental crime?

According to article 33 of Law No. 12,305 / 2010 (National Policy on Solid Waste - PNRS), the manufacturer is, obliged to carry out the reverse logistics of the electronic products it sells. That is, it is the owner's responsibility to look for the manufacturer, who has obligation to collect and dispose, of in an ecologically correct manner. To comply with the Law, companies must implement reverse logistics systems for the collection and recycling of electronics or proper disposal. Likewise, the PNRS provides sanctions both for the company that does not comply with the law, and for the consumer who unduly dispenses with it.

Legislation


The National Congress decrees:

Art. 1 This Law establishes rules for the management and final destination of electronic products and components, considered as technological waste.
Art. 2. Electronic waste must receive an appropriate final destination that does not causes damage or negative impacts to the environment and society.
Art. 3. E-waste is understood to be all material waste produced by the disposal of electronic equipment for domestic, industrial, commercial and services, which are in disuse and subject to final disposal.
Art. 4 The responsibility for the final destination of electronic waste is joint and several between those responsible for the production, marketing and import of the product and its electronic components.
Art. 5. The final disposal of environmentally appropriate electronic waste will be given through:
- recycling processes and use of the product or its components for their original or other purpose;
- total or partial reuse of its technological components;
- proper final disposal and neutralization of its technological components equated to chemical waste.
§1 The final disposal of electronic waste must comply with environmental legislation, of public health and safety, respecting the normative instructions of the responsible public bodies.
§2 The final destination of electronic equipment and components that contain heavy metals or toxic substances must be preceded by environmental license from the competent body, which may require the performance of environmental impact studies for authorization.
Art. 6 The company responsible for manufacturing, importing or marketing of electro-electronic technological products must maintain collection points to receive waste electronic to be discarded by the consumer. Single paragraph. After collecting the e-waste, the person responsible must promote its environmentally appropriate final destination, in accordance with legislation health and safety.
Art. 7 It is incumbent upon the Public Power to oversee the final destination of electronic waste, as provided in this law.
Art. 8. Failure to comply with the provisions of this law shall subject the offender to the following penalties:
I - warning
II - fine
III - prohibition to manufacture, import or sell product or component electronics subject to the rules of this law.
§1 In case of recurrence, the amount of the fine will be doubled and the penalty aggravated.
Art. 9. It is strictly prohibited to the remittance, shipping, sale, disposal and traffic of electronic waste between countries, states and municipalities.
§1 It is incumbent upon the competent Public Authority to inspect and prevent this type of crime, with the adoption of the necessary measures.
§2 Compliance with the provisions of the caput of this article is considered an obligation to relevant environmental interest, for the purposes of art. 68 of Law no. 9,605, of 1998, without prejudice to the application of other applicable sanctions in the criminal and administrative sphere.
Art. 10 The amounts collected with the fines arising from this law will be destined for selective collection programs and proper disposal of waste electronics.
Art. 11 In order to comply with the provisions of this law, the execution of agreements with cooperatives or associations of collectors and other organized entities of civil society.
Art. 12 The provisions of Law no. 12,305 of 2010 - National Solid Waste Policy.
Art. 13 This Law takes effect one hundred and eighty days after its publication.

In recent years, with increasingly rapid technological innovation and the popularization of electronics products, more and more people acquire new computers, televisions, cell phones and household appliances, generating a serious environmental problem: electronic waste or technological waste. These are thousands of tons of garbage that are produced daily in the Country, from the waste resulting from the disposal of electronic equipment. The growth of this waste multiplies at the accelerated pace of industrial production of electronics, which, every year, launches new and sophisticated equipment in the consumer market. E-waste when discarded incorrectly can pose serious risks to the environment and the health of the population. This is due to the use of heavy metals, highly toxic, in the composition of these equipments. Among such metals, the most common are mercury, beryllium and lead. In addition, there are several other chemical components that can be harmful to the environment. When electronic products are disposed of incorrectly, they end up being absorbed by the soil they came into contact with, also contaminating, later, the water table. Burning these residues is also not adequate, as it releases toxins extremely dangerous to human health in the atmosphere.

Electronic waste already represents 5% of all waste produced by humanity. While this number does not seem alarming, it represents around 50 million tons of garbage, thrown away annually. However, only 6.5 million were treated in an ideal way, according to a study by the United Nations University – UNU. The lack of proper disposal of this waste has created another problem: the traffic of electronic waste between countries. The existence of thousands of dumps, mainly in poorer countries like Ghana, Haiti, and even Brazil, which they accept the deposit of what they say is fertilizer, which is actually waste ash electronics. The problem lies in the difficulty in monitoring the flow of these residues. Scheduled Obsolescence and E-waste

Scheduled obsolescence, also called planned obsolescence, is a technique used by manufacturers to force the purchase of new products, even if the ones you already have are in perfect working condition. It consists of manufacturing items that already have the duration of their useful life previously established.

This concept emerged between 1929 and 1930, against the backdrop of the Great Depression, and had the objective of encouraging a market model based on mass production and consumption, in order to recover the economies of countries in that period. Something similar happens nowadays, when credit is facilitated and governments encourage consumption. An emblematic case of this practice was the formation of the Phoebus Cartel, which, headquartered in Geneva, had the participation of the main lamp manufacturers in Europe and the United States and proposed a reduction in costs and life expectancy of 2.5 thousand lamps. hours to a thousand hours. Planned obsolescence is directly related to the increase in the amount of electronic waste generated and incorrectly disposed of, which harms both the environment and human health.


E-waste: a serious problem in the modern world

Electrical and electronic equipment have several toxic components in their structures. If disposed of incorrectly, these toxic wastes can contaminate the soil and groundwater, putting public health at risk. According to the Mineral Technology Center (CETEM), around 70% of the heavy metals found in controlled dumps and landfills come from incorrectly disposed electronic equipment.

A study by the Ann Arbor Ecology Center researched 36 cell phones of different makes and models, analyzing the amount of toxic components present in the devices, such as lead, bromine and cadmium. These are elements that, from their extraction to the end of the product's useful life, cause potential damage to the environment and human health.


Electronic waste and the environment

With the popularization of mobile telephony and personal personnel, the generation of electronic waste has increased a lot, as buying a new device is often simpler than repairing it, in addition to encouraging programmed obsolescence, practiced by manufacturers to encourage consumers to Always exchange your devices for new ones, usually once a year (or at a shorter interval). Some elements contained in electronic waste, although biodegradable, can remain in the soil for centuries, contaminating everything in the meantime. Noble materials such as gold, silver, copper and platinum, as well as rare metals, are difficult to obtain in nature. It is always preferable for the user to discard the correct devices.


What are the main environmental impacts of electronic waste?

In general, the main damage caused by electronic waste to the environment can be divided into three major groups. Are they:
Reduction in the useful life of landfills
Electronic equipment such as computers and cell phones contain large amounts of materials that take a long time to decompose naturally, such as glass and plastic. When discarded in landfills, these materials increase the volume of waste on site and reduce their useful life, causing even more environmental impact.


Heavy metal contamination
Boards and other electronic equipment circuits contain significant amounts of heavy metals — especially mercury, lead and cadmium. This is one of the main environmental damages caused by electronic waste to the environment, as they are highly polluting substances that affect both the quality of soil and water, rivers and groundwater.
Harm to public health
Although it is not an environmental consequence per se, this problem is directly related to the incorrect disposal of electronic waste as the pollution caused by incorrect disposal can damage the health of the population living around landfills or who live from the separation of destined waste to them.


How important is correct disposal?

To avoid damage caused by electronic waste, there is no other way than to invest in the correct disposal and recycling programs that take into account all people and institutions involved in the useful life of the equipment.
At the governmental level, an important step was taken with the installation of the National Solid Waste Policy and the Computer Reconditioning and Recycling Centers (CRCs), implemented by the Federal Government. But these initiatives are not enough. It is necessary that tax incentives be given to companies that carry out the correct disposal and avoid damage caused by electronic waste to the environment, since this is a costly process, especially because many companies that supply this equipment do not yet have logistics programs Really functional reverse.
In addition, disposal stations should be more accessible to the population as a whole, since today there are few places where the correct disposal of electronic waste can be done.
